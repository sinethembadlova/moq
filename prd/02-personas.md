# Personas

## Overview

MOQ serves two primary groups: the buyers who pool demand and the suppliers who fulfill it. Understanding each group's motivations, constraints, and goals is essential to designing a platform that works for both sides of the market.

## Buyer

A buyer is an SME — a spaza shop, salon, restaurant, school, reseller, or similar small business — that needs to purchase stock but cannot meet a supplier's MOQ on its own. They are price-sensitive, margin-conscious, and accustomed to paying more than they should because they have no alternative route to wholesale pricing. They may not have reliable cash flow, which means committing funds to a campaign carries real risk; the all-or-nothing model directly addresses this by refunding pledges from the campaign wallet if a campaign fails.

Before participating, a buyer must complete KYC verification. This is not a bureaucratic hurdle — it is the trust mechanism that makes the platform viable. Buyers are committing real funds alongside other businesses they have never met, and suppliers are accepting consolidated orders from a platform rather than known counterparties. KYC underpins both relationships.

Buyers interact with the platform by funding a wallet, browsing active campaigns, and pledging from their available wallet balance toward deals that match their stock needs. When a buyer wants a product not yet available on the platform, they can submit a campaign request as a demand signal. Buyers can also invite other buyers they know — particularly those in the same line of business — to join campaigns they are interested in, helping campaigns close faster through existing trade relationships. Their primary motivation is accessing the same prices and suppliers that larger competitors use, without needing to carry the full order themselves. A secondary motivation, as they use the platform repeatedly, is the purchasing intelligence it provides: knowing what deals are available, what other businesses are buying, and what wholesale prices actually look like.

## Supplier

A supplier is a manufacturer, wholesaler, or distributor that sets MOQ thresholds to protect their fulfillment economics. They are typically equipped to serve large buyers but lack a scalable channel to aggregate demand from smaller ones. The platform solves a real problem for them: it delivers consolidated, guaranteed orders without requiring the supplier to manage relationships with hundreds of small buyers individually.

Suppliers must complete a KYC verification process before any of their deals can go live. This can happen proactively — a supplier registers and lists their products — or reactively, when the platform approaches a supplier off the back of demand signals from buyer campaign requests. In both cases, supplier KYC confirms the business is legitimate and capable of fulfilling orders at the stated terms. Once verified, a supplier lists deals and launches campaigns. Their primary motivation is reliable, aggregated order volume from a segment of the market they could not previously serve efficiently. In exchange, they pay the platform a commission on fulfilled order value.
